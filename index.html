Scenario analysis

🍺 Scenario 1

“I need booze for pizza night.”
Clear missionUrgentComplements matter
User mindset

Clear outcome, low patience. Wants speed, availability, delivery ETA, and not forgetting obvious add-ons.

Pain today

Even with clear intent, the user still needs to compare and verify ETA, delivery fee, total price, specific beer-type availability, store coverage, alcohol rules, and whether complementary items are worth adding.

How agentic capability can help
  • Key goal: Make it fast.
  • When users open the alcohol tab, surface quick intent filters such as dinner party, work after hours, or drinks for myself.
  • Show the best predictions and explain why, for example: “Your favorite brand. Arrives in 35 mins and is the cheapest.”

🍿 Scenario 2

“I want some snacks.”
VagueDiscoveryClarify lightly
User mindset

Has a desire, but not a precise product. May be browsing, craving, or buying for others.

Pain today

The user may not even know what to search. Search and filters return a broad shelf, and the user still has to narrow by mood, price, diet, and household fit.

How agentic capability can help
  • Key goal: Smart recommendation.
  • Turn “snacks” into clarification chips: sweet, salty, healthy, spicy, kid-friendly, party, or under a budget.
  • Use LLM-assisted recommendations to generate a small bundle based on mood, diet, household fit, price, and sparse-history signals.
  • Let the user switch quickly to cheaper, healthier, organic, kid-friendly, or party-size alternatives.

🛒 Scenario 3

“Do our weekly grocery run.”
RoutineHouseholdReliability
User mindset

Wants reliability and time savings. This is a household operation, not a discovery moment.

Pain today

Reorder lists are item-based. The user still inspects what extra things they need this time, whether there is a discount or acceptable substitution for some items, what is due, quantity adjustments, and duplicates to remove.

How agentic capability can help
  • Key goal: Fast but correct.
  • Prepare a weekly cart from household cadence, usual staples, local availability, and budget.
  • Highlight what changed this week: extra needs, due items, discounts, substitutions, quantity changes, and duplicates.
  • Let the user confirm substitutions, remove extras, and save useful rules for future weekly carts.

🏢 Scenario 4

“Restock the office for next week’s board meeting within budget.”
Business rulesApprovalsRecurring supplies
User mindset

Wants a low-effort, business-safe order. The buyer cares less about browsing and more about getting the right supplies, on time, with the right approvals and documentation.

Pain today

Business ordering requires estimating quantities, coordinating delivery timing, staying within budget, following approval / expense rules, and avoiding missing supplies. The work is repetitive, policy-bound, and easy to get wrong.

How agentic capability can help
  • Key goal: turn a business need into a policy-ready order.
  • Turn a vague business need into a policy-ready order: estimate quantities from headcount or past orders, recommend the right delivery window, check budget and buyer permissions, flag items that need approval, suggest compliant alternatives when something is out of policy or unavailable, and prepare receipt, invoice, and export details for admin review.
★

Trust challenge: explain → steer → learn

Users will wonder whether the assistant selected the cheapest store, missed a promotion, prioritized sponsored items, or chose the wrong brands. The agent needs proactive explanation, clear controls, sponsored labeling, and confirmation rules.

What the AI should proactively surface

Cart-level rationale
What this cart is for, who it is sized for, and what it optimized for: price, usual brands, delivery speed, fewer substitutions, calories, organic, or event completeness.
Store/vendor rationale
Why this store was selected versus alternatives: total landed cost, item availability, delivery ETA, fees, promotions, and substitution risk.
Flagged item explanations
Explain unusual, expensive, substituted, sponsored, added-as-essential, or non-usual items. Do not explain every item by default.
Feedback prompts with frequency control
Ask why only when the signal is high-value: removal, swap, repeated rejection, quantity edit, or post-checkout rule capture. Limit prompts and stop asking repeated questions.
Quick steering
Users also need quick controls such as choose cheaper store, prioritize fastest delivery, or use usual brands when AI selection is not quite right. Also make it easy to give AI instructions to update the selection, such as supporting voice mode.

Discovery challenge: Preserve browsing, discovery, and upsell

Agentic shopping reduces effort, but it can also remove the browsing moments where users discover new needs, seasonal items, promotions, and add-ons. The agent should reduce required browsing, not eliminate inspired browsing — and proactively recommend relevant add-ons, alternatives, or discovery items when they improve the mission.

1. Separate autopilot from inspiration mode

For chores like milk, toiletries, weekly staples, or “make this cheaper,” users want speed and correctness. For snacks, meals, parties, seasonal shopping, or “treat myself” moments, the assistant should act more like a curated shopping companion.

2. Create micro-intents, not just add-ons

Instead of generic upsell, create relevant next-step intents: “Want drinks that pair with the pizza?”, “Want a healthier snack version?”, or “Want to make this party cart kid-friendly?”

3. Use mission-completion add-ons

Add-ons should be organized by role in the mission: pizza night → drinks, ice, dips, dessert, paper plates; housewarming → serving supplies, cleanup, vegetarian options, backup snacks.

4. Add discovery after the core cart is ready

Finish the core job first, then offer lightweight discovery: “Your core cart is ready. Want to browse a few extras?” Chips can include show deals, add dessert, make it party-ready, try something new, or show local brands.

5. Preserve visual browsing inside the agent flow

The experience should not become only chat bubbles. Keep product cards, mini shelves, swipeable alternatives, bundles, reason chips, “more like this,” and comparison rows.

6. Make promotions feel like discovery, not ads

Promotions should be tied to user intent and explained clearly: “Your usual brand is on sale,” “Similar item, lower unit price,” or “Sponsored, but on promotion and relevant to this cart.”

7. Support user-controlled deal and discovery agents

Let users opt into discovery over time: “Tell me when my usual diapers are on sale,” “Watch for wine deals under $20,” or “Show me new high-protein snacks once a week.”

8. Measure discovery quality, not just upsell

Track add-on acceptance, discovery shelf engagement, new-product trial, repurchase after discovery, sponsored removal, basket expansion without complaint increase, vendor exposure distribution, and dismissal rate.

Principle: Agentic commerce should compress the boring part of shopping, not the enjoyable part. It should complete the mission fast, then offer optional, visual, personalized discovery that creates new intent without feeling like forced upsell.

Other challenges

Protect marketplace ecosystem health

If the agent over-optimizes for cheapest price, fastest ETA, largest brands, or highest-sponsored bids, demand can concentrate too much.

Ranking should monitor vendor income, exposure distribution, supplier diversity, and long-term marketplace health. Because AI-mediated recommendations can be sensitive to position, labels, endorsements, and model changes, Instacart should continuously audit seller visibility and demand concentration. Recommendations should also be fair and explainable to vendors at a high level.

Make context decision-ready, not exhaustive

A user can have thousands of behavioral signals, but only a few matter in any given task.

The agent should retrieve a compact, task-specific context packet.

Prevent proactive help from feeling intrusive

Proactive prompts should appear only in high-confidence moments: broad search, party-like cart, repeated browsing without adding, missing essentials, or recurring grocery cadence.

Learn from feedback without annoying users

Ask for feedback at high-signal moments with frequency caps. The goal is to infer preferences from behavior and ask for confirmation only when it materially improves future shopping.

Retailer shopping assistant

Instacart can become the operating layer for retailer-owned shopping assistants: flexible enough for each retailer’s business rules, structured enough to be reliable, and powerful enough to connect AI conversations to real commerce actions.

The differentiation is not white-label chat; it is the full commerce operating layer behind the assistant: data readiness, tool calling, eval, policy controls, checkout, and continuous improvement.

Challenges

The core challenge is balancing shopper trust, retailer business goals, and operational reliability.

Conflict between UX and business demands

Retailer goals may conflict with user goals across upsell, sponsorship, margin, price, speed, quality, and usual-brand preference. The platform needs to build trust, protect the customer experience, and reach a good balance between user value and retailer business goals.

Customized retailer requirements

The platform needs to be flexible enough to support different retailer priorities and prioritize the highest-impact demands for each business.

Uneven retailer data readiness

The algorithm should be shared, but each retailer may have different user graph quality, catalog attributes, inventory freshness, loyalty APIs, and checkout reliability.

The platform needs data adapters, readiness scoring, and fallback modes: use session intent and mission templates when data is sparse, and use deeper user / product graphs when the retailer’s data is mature.

Improve the model without using retailer data

Retailers may block training shared model weights on private data.

The platform needs other improvement paths: shared grocery intelligence, synthetic tasks, privacy-safe failure patterns, retailer-specific evals, prompt / tool improvements, ranking-policy tuning, and runtime retrieval instead of training on private retailer data.

Strategic recommendation

Position the B2B2C assistant product as a full lifecycle AI operations platform for retailers, not a white-label chatbot.

Rollout packaging

Retailers should be able to start with one surface, such as search assistant, cart assistant, loyalty assistant, recipe-to-cart, or post-order assistant, then expand into a full shopping assistant once ROI and operational reliability are proven.

Recommended charge model

Use a hybrid outcome-based + usage-based model. Define the core paid outcome as checkout success from an assistant-assisted session.

Keep usage-based pricing for infrastructure costs.

Partnership with LLMs

Vision: Instacart should be the trusted grocery decision layer for LLM shopping — turning AI-generated intent into reliable, checkout-ready grocery outcomes.

Strategic role

The opportunity is to help LLMs move from planning to execution, making LLM-originated shopping accurate, explainable, and ready to buy. The strategic risk is that LLM partners own the user relationship and ranking layer while Instacart becomes an invisible fulfillment endpoint. Instacart’s defense is to expose a superior grocery decision layer, not just catalog access.

2

Focus: Best grocery context and ranking interaction layer

The hard problem is that LLM partners may own the outer conversation, retrieval, and ranking. Instacart still needs to make the final grocery outcome high-quality and trusted — without becoming just a SKU API behind someone else’s shopping experience.

1. Own grocery decision quality

This is where Instacart can be better than a general LLM: not broad language understanding, but grocery-specific decision quality built on deep user understanding, product graph, store graph, inventory, pricing, and fulfillment data.

  • Translate a broad LLM request into a grocery mission: weekly restock, quick dinner, party, budget shop, health goal, refill, or urgent need.
  • Use Instacart’s graph to reason about product role, quantity fit, substitution quality, cart completeness, store tradeoffs, availability, and fulfillment risk.
  • Apply grocery-specific ranking logic that a general LLM may not have: what should be in the cart, what should be substituted, what is risky, and what tradeoff best serves the user.
2. Adapt to LLM-side ranking and memory

Because the LLM may decide what to retrieve, compare, and present, Instacart needs an interface that makes its grocery solution legible to the LLM’s own systems.

  • Accept user-approved LLM memory and planning context, then map it into Instacart’s own grocery decision layer.
  • Return structured outcome signals the LLM can compare: total cost, ETA, availability confidence, substitution risk, cart completeness, and checkout readiness.
  • Return a complete, checkout-ready grocery solution with rationale and constraints, so the LLM can present an optimized outcome instead of inferring grocery truth from raw SKU data.
  • Compete at the solution level against other providers, instead of being ranked as a generic catalog or fulfillment endpoint.
  • Make Instacart’s offer structured, comparable, and auditable across LLM partners and model versions.
2

Focus: Trust and control

This is the same trust problem as the Commerce within Instacart experience, but with one extra challenge: the user may be shopping through an LLM surface instead of Instacart’s owned app.

Make the result explainable

Instacart should provide the reasoning layer behind the cart so the LLM can explain the grocery decision accurately, including the key tradeoffs, confidence level, and constraints behind the recommendation.

Support control in both UI models

If Instacart can control the UI, quick steering is critical: users should be able to adjust the cart, compare tradeoffs, and confirm risky choices without restarting the flow.

If the LLM controls the UI, Instacart should still generate strong alternatives — or provide the signals that help the LLM generate them — so users can compare safe, accurate, and adjustable grocery choices.
